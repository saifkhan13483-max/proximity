How to Remove Collections from Your Credit Report

By Diana Reeves · January 15, 2025 · 8 min read

Collection accounts are one of the most damaging items that can appear on your credit report. A single collection account can drop your credit score by 50 to 100 points, depending on your overall credit profile. The good news? Collections can often be removed — and knowing the right approach makes all the difference.

Understanding Collection Accounts

A collection account appears on your credit report when a creditor has given up trying to collect a debt and sells or transfers the account to a third-party collection agency. The original debt might be a medical bill, a credit card, a utility account, or virtually any other type of credit obligation.

Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), collection accounts can legally remain on your credit report for seven years from the date of first delinquency. However, many collection accounts contain errors — wrong amounts, wrong dates, duplicate entries — that give you the legal right to dispute them.

"The FCRA gives every American the right to accurate credit reporting. Inaccurate information must be corrected or removed — period. That's not just our policy; it's federal law."

Step 1: Get Your Free Credit Reports

Before you can dispute anything, you need to know exactly what's on your reports. Visit AnnualCreditReport.com to get free reports from Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. Review every collection account carefully, noting the creditor name, account number, balance, and date of first delinquency.

What to Look For

Common errors in collection accounts include: incorrect balances (often inflated with fees), wrong dates (especially the "date opened" or "date of last activity"), duplicate entries for the same debt, accounts you don't recognize at all, and debts that have passed the seven-year reporting limit.

Step 2: Send a Debt Validation Letter

If the collection is recent (within 30 days of first contact), you have the right under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) to request debt validation. Send a certified letter to the collection agency demanding they prove the debt is valid and that they have the legal right to collect it. Many collection agencies — especially those who've purchased old debt — cannot provide proper documentation, and the account must be removed.

Step 3: File a Dispute with Each Bureau

For any collection account that contains an error, you have the right to dispute it directly with the credit bureaus. Each bureau has an online dispute portal, or you can send a certified dispute letter. Your dispute should clearly identify the account, state specifically what is incorrect, and include any supporting documentation you have.

The bureau must investigate your dispute within 30 days. If the collection agency cannot verify the information, the bureau is required by law to remove the account from your report.

Step 4: Negotiate a Pay-for-Delete Agreement

If the collection is valid but you want to remove it, you may be able to negotiate a "pay-for-delete" agreement with the collection agency. In exchange for payment (often a settlement for less than the full balance), the agency agrees in writing to delete the account from your credit reports. Get this agreement in writing before making any payment.

Step 5: Dispute Again If Necessary

If your initial dispute is rejected, don't give up. You can re-dispute with additional evidence, file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), or request the method of verification the bureau used. Persistence pays off — many collections are removed on the second or third attempt.

The process of removing collections is detailed and time-consuming, but it's absolutely worth the effort. Every collection removed can significantly improve your credit score and expand your financial opportunities.
